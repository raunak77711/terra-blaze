Budget Trekking in Nepal: How to Do It Without Breaking the Bank

By Bimal Rai · June 22, 2026 · 7 Min Read

Travel Tips • June 22, 2026 • 7 Min Read

Nepal is one of the most affordable adventure destinations in the world — if you know how to plan. This guide breaks down every major cost category so you can trek smart without cutting corners on safety.

Typical Daily Budget (Teahouse Trek)
  • Budget: USD 25–35/day (basic teahouses, dal bhat meals)
  • Mid-range: USD 40–60/day (better rooms, varied menu)
  • Comfortable: USD 70–100/day (best available rooms, guided)

For most popular treks (EBC, Annapurna, Langtang), total permit costs run USD 35–50. The Manaslu and Upper Mustang are restricted areas with significantly higher fees.

Teahouse Accommodation

The teahouse system makes Nepal incredibly affordable compared to camping destinations. A basic room costs NPR 200–500 (USD 1.50–4) per night in most areas, though this often comes with an unspoken expectation that you eat your meals at the same teahouse — and that's where the economics work out.

The Real Deal: Many teahouses offer free or discounted rooms if you commit to eating all meals there. Always eat where you sleep to support your host — and your wallet.

Food on the Trail

Dal bhat — lentil soup, rice, vegetable curry — is the staple trekking meal and the best budget choice. At NPR 400–600 (USD 3–5), it comes with unlimited refills and is the most nutritionally complete option on the menu. As you gain altitude, prices for all food items increase by 30–50% due to transport costs.

  • Dal Bhat: NPR 400–700 (best value, unlimited refills)
  • Breakfast (porridge/eggs): NPR 200–400
  • Hot drinks (tea/coffee): NPR 100–300
  • Bottled water: NPR 150–500 (use purification tabs instead to save NPR and plastic)
  • Snickers/energy bars: NPR 200–400 each above 4,000 m

Guide vs. Porter vs. Solo

Hiring a Guide (Recommended)

A licensed local guide costs USD 25–35/day, including their food, accommodation, and equipment allowance. For first-time trekkers, this is money extraordinarily well spent — your guide handles permits, navigation, teahouse negotiation, and is your first line of response in a medical emergency.

Hiring a Porter

A porter carries up to 20 kg and costs USD 15–20/day. Porter welfare is a serious ethical issue — ensure your agency (and you) provide proper insurance, appropriate clothing, and fair rest periods. TerraBlaze is a signatory of the International Porter Protection Group's guidelines.

Solo Trekking

Solo trekking is permitted on most major trails (and fully banned on Manaslu, Upper Mustang, Dolpo — these require a guide). It saves money on guide fees but means you navigate alone, negotiate teahouses alone, and have no one to help in an emergency. We recommend at minimum a porter-guide for solo first-timers.

Never skip travel insurance to save money. A single helicopter evacuation costs more than a full month of quality trekking. Budget for it as a non-negotiable line item.

Getting There: Flights & Transport

  • Kathmandu → Lukla flight (EBC): USD 170–220 each way with domestic airlines
  • Kathmandu → Pokhara flight (Annapurna): USD 80–120; bus NPR 800–1,500 (8 hrs)
  • Local jeep/bus to trailhead: NPR 500–2,000 depending on route
  • Tourist bus Kathmandu–Pokhara: USD 12–18
